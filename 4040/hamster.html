Hamster

The P/C-69 ship core first entered service in 1971. Despite being a first generation core, it proved very successful and was retrofitted numerous times. It's powerful reactor, generous crew compartment and vast compatibility with all kinds of engines and payloads meant it could be used for all kinds of combat, patrol and even civilian applications by numerous clients. The later version (P/C-69 U2) changed internal structure to make more efficient use of internal space and replaced the reactor with a slightly weaker, but more efficient one, as well as modernizing the armor composition. Both versions severely suffer from travel range, making them widely unsuited to engage in offensive combat, unless fitted with external drop-tanks. The early nuclear reactor was also very maintenance heavy and unreliable, requiring frequent maintenance and constant proximity to port or a mothership. P/C-69 s can still be seen used today for search and rescue, specialized utility vehicles and by some paramilitary organizations.

Famous example

The Space Hamster depicted here was a missile corvette used by the Lunar navy as part of a larger battle group in the early 2000's to defend stations on the L2 Earth-Sun Lagrange point. It was most notable in the 2011 John Carver Mutiny, were the crew of 8 displayed great skills in disabling a small carrier and other corvette as well as surviving in a badly damaged and decompressed ship for a week until saved. The ship is now on display as a museum ship around Luna.
